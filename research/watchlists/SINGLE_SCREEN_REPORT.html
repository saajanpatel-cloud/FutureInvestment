What this pack covers. The dashboard scores a growth-oriented universe on a consistent rubric, runs multiple valuation models, stress-tests each thesis, and surfaces names for deeper work. Sections follow one pipeline: Discover (goals and weights), Screen (universe and shortlist), Research (adversarial reviews), Value (scenarios, DCF, risk metrics, Monte Carlo), Decide (reconciliation), then Monitor in the live HTML (charts, watchlist, triggers — screen-only in the PDF export).

How names are scored. Each company is scored 1–5 on six dimensions — growth, margins, balance sheet, durability, tail risks (higher is worse), and valuation (higher means cheaper). Scores combine into a composite out of 30 with equal weights and transparent qualitative criteria — no hidden weighting or black-box scoring.

Evidence and models. Fundamentals and history come primarily from yfinance (verify material numbers in SEC filings and company releases). Valuation layers include forward-multiple scenarios, DCF sensitivity grids, historical risk metrics, and Monte Carlo paths. The live HTML adds filters, sorting, TradingView charts, and directional social inputs from Reddit and X — not trading signals. AI assists theme research, earnings cross-checks, adversarial review, and sentiment scanning; every buy/sell/hold decision stays with you.

AI infrastructure & compute 27%

Chips, servers, data centres, and the gear that runs AI.

AI workloads — training large models, running inference at scale, and deploying enterprise copilots — need specialised hardware at every layer. That starts with GPUs and accelerators (the processors designed for parallel computation), but extends to high-bandwidth memory (the fast storage chips bolted next to processors), networking switches (the gear that connects thousands of chips inside a data centre), cooling systems (liquid and air solutions to remove heat from dense racks), and power delivery equipment. Big cloud companies like Amazon, Microsoft, and Google publish multi-year capital spending plans that run into tens of billions of dollars per quarter. That money flows down through a supply chain of chip designers, wafer equipment makers, packaging specialists, and infrastructure vendors — many of which are less well-known than the headline platform stocks.

  • What drives demand: Training and running AI models at scale; enterprise adoption of copilots and agents; sovereign AI programs where governments want domestic compute capacity; data-centre buildout for cloud workloads beyond AI.
  • Where value accrues: GPU and accelerator designers (NVDA, AMD); memory makers (MU); chip-design software or EDA tools (SNPS); wafer fabrication equipment or WFE — the machines that manufacture chips (LRCX, AMAT, KLAC); data-centre networking (ANET); power and cooling (VRT). Each layer has different margins, competition, and cyclicality.
  • How to learn: Read the "segment revenue" and "customer concentration" sections in 10-K filings. A company might report headline growth, but if 60% of revenue comes from two hyperscaler customers, that concentration is a risk. Also compare capex plans from cloud companies quarter to quarter — they signal future demand for the supply chain.
  • Common mistake: Treating every software company with "AI" in its pitch as an AI infrastructure play. The real test is whether the company's revenue comes from selling the physical or design tools that make AI work, or whether it is just adding AI features to an existing product.

Energy transition & grids 26%

Power plants, grids, and equipment that keep the lights on as electricity demand rises.

AI is not only a software story — it is increasingly a physics and infrastructure story. A single large data centre can draw as much power as a small city. That means more baseload generation (gas, nuclear, or renewables running around the clock), more peak capacity, upgrades to transmission lines and substations, and investment in energy storage. 'Energy transition' here covers the whole system: from the power plant to the socket, including the companies that build, maintain, and operate it. Regulated utilities (whose returns are set by regulators) sit alongside merchant power producers (whose profits depend on market electricity prices) and renewable developers — each with different risk profiles.

  • What drives demand: Electrification of transport and industry; data-centre load growth (estimated to double or triple in many grids by 2030); grid modernisation to handle variable renewables; government policy incentives for clean energy.
  • Where value accrues: Regulated utilities earn returns set by their regulator — slower but steadier (NEE, XEL, WEC, SO, DUK, NGG, SSE.L). Independent power producers or IPPs earn market prices — more volatile (VST, CEG). Solar module manufacturers (FSLR) and inverter makers (ENPH) depend on project pipelines and policy. Midstream pipeline operators (ET) move gas and liquids. Grid equipment companies sell transformers, switchgear, and HVDC links — often with multi-year backlogs (ENR.DE). Small modular nuclear reactors represent a new power source for data centres (RR.L).
  • How to learn: Read utility IRPs (integrated resource plans) — these are public documents showing how a utility plans to meet load over the next 10–20 years. Check interconnection queues (how many new projects are waiting to connect to the grid). Compare company commentary on data-centre contracts versus merchant exposure. When nuclear headlines or video coverage spike (SMR, baseload, data-centre power deals), extract tickers only as a watch list, then verify each against IRPs, licence milestones, and revenue mix the same way you would for any IPP or utility.
  • Common mistake: Confusing long-run load growth (a structural trend that takes years to play out) with short-run power prices (which can spike or crash because of weather, gas prices, or policy changes unrelated to your thesis).

Cybersecurity & digital trust 9%

Software and services that stop hacks and protect data.

More cloud workloads and AI systems mean a wider attack surface — more endpoints, more APIs, more data moving between services. At the same time, regulators and boards are raising the bar on security baselines. That makes parts of cyber spending behave like recurring infrastructure: companies renew security subscriptions the way they pay for electricity. The sector includes endpoint protection (software on laptops and servers), cloud workload security (protecting apps running in the cloud), network detection, identity management (controlling who can access what), and vulnerability scanning. Revenue is typically subscription-based, so the key metrics are renewal rates and net revenue retention — how much existing customers spend over time.

  • What drives demand: Headline-grabbing breaches and ransomware attacks that remind boards to spend; zero-trust architecture adoption (the idea that no user or device is trusted by default); identity for humans and machines; OT security for factories and utilities that are increasingly connected.
  • Where value accrues: Platform vendors trying to consolidate multiple security tools into one stack (PANW, CRWD); network security appliance makers with installed bases (FTNT, CHKP); zero-trust and SASE specialists (ZS); identity providers (OKTA); niche vulnerability and exposure management (QLYS, TENB). Each has different margin structures and growth rates.
  • How to learn: Compare ARR growth, net retention rate, and free cash flow margin across peers. Check whether a vendor is gaining "module attach" — selling additional products to existing customers. Read quarterly earnings for billings vs revenue cadence (billings often lead revenue by a quarter or two).
  • Common mistake: Assuming all cyber stocks move together. Net retention and module adoption differ sharply by vendor — one company growing 30% can sit next to a peer growing 10% in the same "cyber" bucket.

Industrial automation & robotics 11%

Factories and robots getting more automated.

Labour shortages, rising quality standards, and the reshoring of manufacturing all push factories toward more automation. This theme covers the full stack: physical robots that weld, assemble, and pack; motion control systems (motors, drives, actuators); sensors that give machines eyes and touch; industrial software that designs, simulates, and monitors production lines; and the integrators that tie everything together. The spending is cyclical — it rises when companies invest in new capacity and falls during downturns — so understanding order books and backlog quality matters as much as headline revenue growth.

  • What drives demand: Capital expenditure cycles in automotive, logistics, and electronics manufacturing; nearshoring (moving production closer to end markets); safety and precision requirements in food, pharma, and semiconductor fabs; labour shortages in ageing economies.
  • Where value accrues: Factory automation and software (ROK, SIE.DE); electrical power management for data centres and factories (ETN, SU.PA); robotics and electrification (ABB); diversified industrials with automation segments (HON, EMR); heavy machinery with services attach (CAT, DE); motion control and hydraulics (PH); climate and HVAC systems (TT, IR, ITW). Recurring services revenue is more durable than one-time equipment sales.
  • How to learn: Track book-to-bill ratios and order backlogs in quarterly filings. Check how much of revenue is recurring services versus one-time equipment. Look for "automation" or "digital" segments broken out in financial statements.
  • Common mistake: Confusing order growth with durable profit. Large backlogs can reverse quickly when customers cut capex in a downturn. Also, many "automation" conglomerates have large legacy segments unrelated to the theme — always check what percentage of revenue is actually automation.

Health Tech 14%

Tech-driven healthcare — robotic surgery, AI diagnostics, biotech pipelines, structural heart devices.

This is not broad healthcare. Quantum / frontier compute 5%

Very early-stage quantum and next-gen compute bets.

Quantum computing aims to solve specific classes of problems — molecular simulation, optimisation, cryptography — by harnessing quantum mechanics. But useful commercial scale (where a quantum computer reliably solves a real business problem faster and cheaper than a classical computer) remains uneven. Most pure-play quantum stocks are pre-revenue or minimal-revenue, with high cash burn, frequent equity raises (dilution), and valuations driven more by narrative than fundamentals. The sleeve's 10% weight treats this as long-dated optionality: you are buying a lottery ticket on a technology that could be transformative, with the understanding that most of these companies may not survive in their current form. Adjacent 'frontier compute' includes the control electronics, cryogenics systems, and software stacks that quantum hardware needs — each with different risk profiles.

  • What drives interest: National competitiveness (governments funding quantum programs); the coming need to migrate encryption to post-quantum standards (NIST PQC timelines); partnerships between quantum startups and major cloud providers offering 'quantum as a service.'
  • Where value may accrue (eventually): Quantum hardware (trapped-ion, superconducting, photonic — competing approaches with no clear winner yet); control and cryogenic systems; error-correction and compiler software; cloud access platforms. IBM is the exception — quantum is a tiny fraction of a large services and mainframe business, giving it a different risk profile from pure plays.
  • How to learn: Read S-1 and 10-K risk factors carefully — they often explain the company's own view of how far away commercial viability is. Check cash runway (how many quarters until they need to raise again). Look at revenue quality: is it government grants, consulting, or actual product sales? When quantum is loud on YouTube or conference circuits, mine mentioned tickers only as hypotheses, then reconcile each name to the latest filings, funding rounds, and partnership disclosures before you add or swap a row in this universe.
  • Common mistake: Equating scientific milestones (a paper showing fewer errors, a new qubit record) with near-term per-share cash flows. A breakthrough in the lab does not mean revenue next quarter. Many names are dilution-heavy, meaning your ownership percentage shrinks with each equity raise.

Methodology notes — forward-multiple scenarios

Purpose. This block is a structured stress test, not a forecast. It answers: “If earnings (or sales) grew at X% for three years and the market paid Y times at the end, what price would that imply?” Use it to compare names on the same yardstick and to see how sensitive implied value is to growth and multiple.

Data source. Inputs are pulled from yfinance as of the generation timestamp on this page. Forward EPS uses analyst next-twelve-month (NTM) consensus where the feed exposes it; otherwise trailing EPS. Pre-profit names use revenue per share as the scaling metric so the grid stays comparable. Always reconcile material inputs to filings and company guidance.

Mechanics. For each scenario row: implied price ≈ metric × (1 + CAGR)³ × exit multiple, where the horizon is three years. The weighted target is the probability-weighted average of the three scenario prices: Σ(pricei × pi). Default probabilities are 25% / 50% / 25% (bull / base / bear). IONQ uses 20% / 45% / 35% to reflect higher outcome dispersion.

How to read Δ%. The percentage is the change from the spot price at export time to the implied scenario price. It is not an expected return statement — it is the mechanical output of the stated CAGR and multiple.

What the model omits. Single line item (EPS or rev/sh); no explicit path for buybacks, dilution, dividends, net debt, or working capital. No segment-level mix. Exit multiples are static scalars, not functions of ROIC or competitive intensity. Do not treat outputs as fair value — treat them as relative illustrations for prioritisation.

Reproducibility. Growth CAGRs and exit multiples are defined in the project scenario assumptions (CSV / generator). If you change a view, change the inputs and regenerate; the report is a snapshot.

Methodology notes — historical risk metrics

Purpose. These statistics summarise how the stock has actually behaved in the recent window versus a liquid US equity benchmark. They inform sizing and liquidity tolerance, not thesis quality. A high historical Sharpe does not validate a business model; a deep drawdown does not prove impairment.

Sample. Two years of adjusted daily closing prices from yfinance, aligned on common trading dates. The benchmark is SPY (S&P 500 ETF) as a broad US risk proxy. Holidays and missing prints are dropped after alignment.

Returns. Simple close-to-close arithmetic returns: rt = (Pt − Pt−1) / Pt−1.

Beta. OLS slope of stock on SPY over the full window: β = Cov(rstock, rSPY) / Var(rSPY). This is a single-regime estimate — it compresses crisis and calm into one number.

Annualised volatility. Sample standard deviation of daily returns × √252 (US equity trading-day convention). This scales realised dispersion to a one-year horizon; it is not a GARCH forecast.

Maximum drawdown. Largest peak-to-trough decline on adjusted closes within the window: maxt[(peak≤t − Pt) / peak≤t]. Path-dependent; two stocks with the same vol can have very different drawdowns.

Sharpe-like ratio. (Annualised arithmetic mean return − 4.5% annual risk-free proxy) / annualised volatility. The 4.5% level approximates a short T-bill yield band for this report snapshot; it is not matched daily to the curve. Negative values imply the stock lost money on average after the crude risk-free adjustment.

Correlation. Pearson correlation of daily returns with SPY. High positive values mean the name tends to move with the broad market; values near zero suggest more idiosyncratic day-to-day noise (or data issues).

Limitations. Entirely backward-looking; corporate actions and vendor restatements can shift history. Survivorship is implicit in “names on the list today.” Regimes change — a calm two-year window can precede a disorderly quarter. Use alongside fundamental tail risks in the Research section, not as a substitute.

Monitoring triggers

Indicators that confirm or weaken the original thesis. Review these quarterly alongside earnings data.

Confirming signals (hold or add)

Revenue beats consensus for 2+ consecutive quarters. Margin expansion continues. Management raises guidance. Order backlog grows. Analyst upgrades with price target increases.

Weakening signals (reduce or reassess)

Revenue misses for 2 consecutive quarters. Guidance cut or withdrawn. Key customer loss announced. Regulatory headwind materialises. Insider selling accelerates beyond vesting schedules.

Exit triggers

Kill criteria from the Research section are met. Cash runway drops below 3 quarters (for pre-profit names). Thesis-breaking event: acquisition, delisting, fraud disclosure, or fundamental business model change.

Appendix: AI Architecture and Data Sources

What powers this platform, where the data comes from, and how to reproduce the analysis

Data sources

yfinance (Python)

Fundamentals: price, market cap, P/E ratios, EPS, revenue, margins, cash flow, analyst targets. Historical price data for risk metrics and Monte Carlo inputs. Free, no API key required. Can be delayed or incorrect — always verify against filings.

TradingView (embedded widget)

Live candlestick charts for visual price action and timing assessment. Loaded client-side via TradingView's public widget API. Requires internet connection.

Reddit and X (directional only)

Finnhub free-tier context: analyst recommendation mix, insider MSPR, recent headline count, earnings timing. Not retail social sentiment.

SEC filings (manual reference)

10-K and 10-Q filings for earnings verification, risk factors, segment breakdowns, and management commentary. Not automatically pulled — used to cross-check yfinance data.

Screening methodology

Every company is scored 1–5 on six dimensions: growth (revenue trajectory), margins (gross and operating margin quality), balance sheet (debt vs cash), durability (competitive moat strength), tail risks (low-probability high-impact threats — higher is worse), and valuation (price relative to fundamentals — higher means cheaper). Scores are equal-weighted into a composite total out of 30. The rubric is qualitative and transparent — no hidden weighting or black-box scoring.

Valuation models

Forward-multiple scenario analysis

Takes current earnings, grows them at three rates (bull / base / bear) over 3 years, applies exit multiples, and probability-weights the results.

DCF sensitivity

Discounted cash flow grids testing 5 growth rates against 5 WACC assumptions. Each cell shows the implied share price and percentage difference from current market price.

Historical risk metrics

Two years of daily price data: beta (market sensitivity), annualised volatility, maximum drawdown, Sharpe ratio, and S&P 500 correlation.

Monte Carlo simulation

10,000 random price paths using geometric Brownian motion. Outputs probability of 50%+ gain and 30%+ loss over the projection period.

AI workflow

Claude supports each stage of the pipeline:

  • Theme research: AI identifies demand drivers, supply chain nodes, and overlooked names within each theme.
  • Earnings analysis: Latest quarterly data pulled via yfinance. AI cross-references against management commentary and flags thesis contradictions.
  • Adversarial review: AI generates structured bear-case bullets, stress points, and kill criteria for each proposed name.
  • Sentiment scanning: Directional social signals aggregated from Reddit and X.

Guardrails

  • Every page includes a disclaimer: this is education and research, not personalised investment advice.
  • No name advances to "proposed" status without a completed adversarial review.
  • All rubric scores require dated, citable evidence — no subjective scoring without data.
  • Human-in-the-loop: the platform surfaces analysis, but the investor makes every buy/sell/hold decision.
  • Data freshness: yfinance data can be delayed or incorrect. Always verify critical numbers in SEC filings before acting.
